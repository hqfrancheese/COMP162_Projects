Machine Learning¶

Question 5: Does the number of genetic ailment affect a breed's lifespan?¶

In [235]:
sns.lmplot(data=df, x='Number Of Genetic Ailments', y='Longevity(Years)')
plt.title('Longevity vs Number Of Genetic Ailments')

X = df[['Number Of Genetic Ailments']]
y = df['Longevity(Years)']

model = LinearRegression()
model.fit(X, y)

y_pred = model.predict(X)

r2 = r2_score(y, y_pred)
rmse = np.sqrt(mean_squared_error(y, y_pred))
               
print(f'Intercept: {model.intercept_}')
print(f'Coefficient: {model.coef_[0]}')
print(f'R2: {r2:.4f}')
print(f'RMSE: {rmse:.4f}')

Intercept: 11.484068978169143
Coefficient: -0.2168299394606495
R2: 0.0365
RMSE: 1.8918

Interpretations:¶

Independent: Number of Genetic Ailements

Dependent: Longevity(Years)

Do dogs with less number of genetic problems tend to live longer? If so, is there a big difference?

The intercept represents the predicted lifespan of a dog with zero ailment to be 11.5 years. Meanwhile the coefficient tells us how much of the lifespan decrease with each added ailment. In this case, each ailment will take off about 0.22 years in a dogs lifespan. Although it shows a negative linear relationship, the R2 score is very low indicating that it is a very weak linear relationship. This could be due to other factors affecting the lifespan of a dog. The RMSE is also 1.89, suggesting that the lifespan predictions are off by 2 years.

Overall, breeds with more ailments still tend to live slightly shorter than those with less. I also probably wouldn't recommend this model in the future just because it might not be reliable for prediction in the real world. The model also seems to be underfitting as it doesn't capture the other existing variables.
